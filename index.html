Case Study Analyses

4.1 Summary of the Incident

In 2023, the PhilHealth Medusa Attack saw hackers from the Medusa ransomware group breach the Philippine Health Insurance Corporation’s systems, stealing sensitive personal information, including confidential memos and member data. The hackers demanded a P17 million ransom, which the government refused, prompting calls for enhanced cybersecurity measures across public and private sectors to prevent similar breaches.

4.2 Ethical Issues Involved

  • Negligence in Data Protection: PhilHealth failed to maintain basic cybersecurity (e.g., expired antivirus), violating their duty of care to safeguard sensitive data. Why it matters: Inadequate security measures expose sensitive data to risks, undermining public trust.
  • Violation of Privacy Rights: The breach compromised personal and health-related information, infringing on individuals' right to privacy under the Data Privacy Act (RA 10173). Why it matters: Privacy is a fundamental right, and its violation erodes personal security.
  • Lack of Transparency: Delayed and limited disclosure of the breach showed a lack of accountability and openness, leaving the public uninformed. Why it matters: Transparency is essential for maintaining public trust and accountability.
  • Public Sector Responsibility: As a government agency, PhilHealth’s failure reflects poor stewardship of public trust and taxpayer-funded resources. Why it matters: Public institutions must uphold higher standards of responsibility.
  • Harm to Vulnerable Populations: The leak disproportionately affected vulnerable citizens who may lack the means to protect themselves from fraud or identity theft. Why it matters: Ethical systems must prioritize protecting the most vulnerable.
  • Malicious Intent of Hackers: The Medusa ransomware group acted unethically by exploiting data for profit, violating the principle of non-maleficence (do no harm). Why it matters: Exploitative actions undermine ethical principles and societal trust.

4.3 Application of Ethical Theories

  • Deontology: PhilHealth had a duty under RA 10173 to secure data, and their failure to do so breached this obligation, causing harm.
  • Virtue Ethics: The lack of diligence and responsibility in maintaining cybersecurity reflects a failure in organizational virtues.
  • Utilitarianism: The breach caused widespread harm to millions, outweighing any cost savings from inadequate security, making it unethical.

4.4 Proposed Ethical Solutions and Interventions

Organizations should conduct regular security audits, implement end-to-end encryption, and train staff on cybersecurity protocols. Compliance with RA 10173, including appointing a Data Protection Officer, is critical. Public awareness campaigns can educate users on phishing risks, reducing vulnerabilities to similar attacks.
